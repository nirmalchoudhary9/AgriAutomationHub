Tomato Diseases

1. Early Blight:

The early blight fungus survives in the soil on residue of infected tomatoes, potatoes, and nightshade weeds. The fungus is spread by spores (oval shaped with a long beak) carried by wind or splashed in water. Germination of spores and infection require free moisture. Disease development stops in dry, hot weather. The early blight fungus survives in the soil on residue of infected tomatoes, potatoes, and nightshade weeds.

2. Late Blight:

Tomato late blight is caused by the oomycete pathogen Phytophthora infestans (P. infestans). The pathogen is best known for causing the devastating Irish potato famine of the 1840s, which killed over a million people, and caused another million to leave the country The first symptoms of late blight on tomato leaves are irregularly shaped, water-soaked lesions, often with a lighter halo or ring around them , these lesions are typically found on the younger, more succulent leaves in the top portion of the plant canopy.

3. Fusarium Wilt:

Fusarium wilt is a devastating soil-borne fungal disease of tomato throughout the United States and worldwide. The disease is caused by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp.lycopersici that can cause significant yield losses of tomato production in greenhouse, high tunnel, and field. Symptoms and diagnostics Fusarium wilt of tomato is a systemic vascular disease. The pathogen enters tomato plants through roots and colonizes the plant’s vascular tissues, which clogs the xylem and causes drooping of leaves . Symptoms generally are noticeable in the mid to late growing season although plants can be infected at any stages of growth.

4.Tomato Bacterial spot:

Tomato bacterial spot is a significant plant disease caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas euvesicatoria. This bacterial pathogen affects tomato plants, causing characteristic symptoms on the leaves, fruit, and stems. Infected tomato plants exhibit small, dark, water-soaked lesions on the leaves, which later develop into angular, necrotic spots with a yellow halo. These lesions can coalesce, leading to widespread defoliation and reduced photosynthetic capacity.

5.Tomato Septoria leaf spot:

Tomato Septoria leaf spot, caused by the fungus Septoria lycopersici, is a common and destructive disease affecting tomato plants. This fungal pathogen primarily targets the leaves, causing small, circular lesions with dark centers and yellow halos. As the disease progresses, the lesions increase in number and size, leading to extensive defoliation.

6.Tomato Spider mites Two spotted spider mite

The two-spotted spider mite (Tetranychus urticae) is a common pest that poses a significant threat to tomato plants. These tiny arachnids feed on the undersides of tomato leaves, extracting cell contents with their piercing-sucking mouthparts. As they feed, they cause stippling on the leaf surface, which appears as small, yellowish spots.

7.Tomato Target Spot:

Tomato target spot, caused by the fungus Corynespora cassiicola, is a foliar disease that affects tomato plants, particularly during warm and humid conditions. This pathogen is known for causing distinctive lesions on the leaves, which resemble targets with concentric rings.

8.Tomato mosaic virus:

Tomato mosaic virus (ToMV) is a highly contagious plant pathogen that poses a significant threat to tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) crops worldwide. This virus belongs to the Tobamovirus genus and is primarily spread through infected plant debris, contaminated tools, and by human contact during cultivation.

Potato Diseases

1. Bacterial Wilt :

Bacterial wilt in potatoes, attributed to the bacterium Ralstonia solanacearum, poses a significant threat to potato crops worldwide. This destructive disease manifests through the invasion of the plant's vascular system, leading to symptoms like wilting, yellowing, and, ultimately, the demise of the infected plant. The bacterium is highly resilient and can persist in soil, water, or contaminated plant material, facilitating its rapid spread.

2. Septorial leaf Spot:

Leaf spot, caused by the fungus Septoria lycopersici, affects a variety of plants, including potatoes. This common foliar disease primarily targets the leaves, appearing as small, dark spots surrounded by a yellow halo. These spots coalesce over time, leading to extensive leaf damage and potential yield loss. The fungus survives on crop debris and soil, with spores spreading through splashing water or wind. Warm and humid conditions create an ideal environment for its proliferation.

3. Common Scab:

Scab is a widespread bacterial disease affecting potatoes, primarily caused by Streptomyces scabies. This soil-borne pathogen thrives in alkaline conditions and presents a challenge to potato growers globally. Unlike fungal diseases, common scab is caused by bacteria that produce toxins leading to the formation of characteristic lesions on the potato tubers. Symptoms of common scab manifest as superficial corky or scaly lesions on the potato's surface, often resembling warts. These lesions can vary in size and color, ranging from small, slightly raised spots to larger, more pronounced scabs.

4.Potato hollow heart:

Potato hollow heart is a physiological disorder that affects potato tubers, causing the development of hollow cavities within the flesh. While it is not caused by a pathogen like bacteria or fungi, hollow heart results from irregularities in tuber growth, typically associated with environmental and cultural factors.

Apple Diseases

1.Apple Black rot:

Apple black rot, caused by the fungus Botryosphaeria obtusa, is a prevalent and destructive disease that affects apple trees worldwide. This pathogen primarily targets the fruit, causing distinctive symptoms that include dark, circular lesions with concentric rings on the surface.These lesions, often surrounded by a yellow halo, lead to the characteristic "bull's-eye" appearance. As the disease progresses, the affected apples undergo a rapid decay process, turning into mummified, shriveled structures that cling to the tree.

2.Apple Cedar apple rust:

Apple cedar apple rust is a fungal disease caused by Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae, a pathogen that requires two host plants to complete its life cycle – apple trees (Malus species) and certain junipers (Juniperus species). This complex disease poses a significant threat to apple orchards, particularly in regions where junipers are abundant. The symptoms of cedar apple rust are diverse and can affect both leaves and fruit.

Grapes Diseases

1.Grape Black rot:

Grape black rot, caused by the fungus Guignardia bidwellii, is a widespread and economically significant disease affecting grapevines. This fungal pathogen can have devastating effects on both table grapes and wine grape varieties. The disease typically manifests during warm and humid weather conditions, making it particularly problematic in regions with such climates.

2.Grape Esca Black Measles:

Grape Esca, commonly referred to as "Black Measles," is a complex and destructive disease affecting grapevines, caused by a combination of fungi within the Phaeoacremonium and Phaeomoniella genera. This disease is particularly challenging for grape growers, as it can lead to significant economic losses due to vine decline and reduced grape yield and quality.

3.Grape Leaf blight:

Grape Leaf Blight, also known as Isariopsis Leaf Spot, is a fungal disease that affects grapevines, caused by the pathogen Isariopsis winthemi. This disease primarily targets the leaves of the grapevine, leading to significant leaf damage and potential reductions in fruit quality and yield. While it is less severe compared to some other grape diseases, it can still pose a threat under certain conditions.

Orange and Strawberry Diseases

1.Orange Haunglongbing Citrus greening:

Orange Huanglongbing (HLB), also known as Citrus Greening, is a devastating and incurable bacterial disease affecting citrus trees, including oranges. The causal agent of this disease is the bacterium Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus, transmitted primarily by the Asian citrus psyllid (Diaphorina citri). HLB poses a severe threat to the citrus industry globally, leading to significant economic losses and impacting citrus production.

2.Strawberry Leaf scorch:

Strawberry Leaf Scorch is a notable disease affecting strawberry plants and is caused by the bacterium Xylella fastidiosa. This bacterial pathogen is responsible for various plant diseases, and in strawberries, it leads to the characteristic symptoms of leaf scorch and decline. Strawberry Leaf Scorch is known to impact various strawberry varieties, making it a concern for strawberry growers in affected regions.

Rice and Tea Diseases

1.Bacterial leaf blight in rice leaf:

Bacterial Leaf Blight (BLB) is a significant disease affecting rice plants and is caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae. This bacterial pathogen poses a threat to rice crops globally, impacting both yield and grain quality. Bacterial Leaf Blight is known for its rapid spread and ability to cause extensive damage under favorable environmental conditions. The symptoms of Bacterial Leaf Blight in rice typically appear during the vegetative and early reproductive stages of the plant.

2.Leaf smut in rice leaf:

Leaf smut in rice is caused by the fungus, Entyloma oryzae. This disease primarily affects the leaves of rice plants, leading to characteristic symptoms that impact the overall health and productivity of the crop. While leaf smut is not as economically significant as some other rice diseases, it can still cause yield losses under favorable conditions.The symptoms of leaf smut typically manifest as small, white to yellowish lesions on the upper surface of the leaves

3.Brown spot in rice leaf:

Brown spot, caused by the fungus Cochliobolus miyabeanus, is a common and economically significant disease affecting rice plants. This fungal pathogen primarily targets the leaves of rice, leading to characteristic symptoms that can result in yield losses if not managed effectively. The symptoms of brown spot in rice initially appear as small, water-soaked lesions on the leaves, usually near the leaf tips. These lesions gradually enlarge and turn brown, with a yellow halo surrounding them.

4.Anthracnose in tea:

Anthracnose is a fungal disease that can affect tea plants (Camellia sinensis), leading to significant economic losses in tea cultivation. The causal agent of anthracnose in tea is typically the fungus Colletotrichum gloeosporioides. This disease primarily impacts the leaves, but it can also affect stems and, in some cases, young shoots.

5.Brown Blight (Colletotrichum camelliae):

In general, Colletotrichum species are known to cause anthracnose in various plants, characterized by the development of lesions on leaves, stems, and fruits. These lesions often have a dark color, ranging from brown to black, and may be associated with a surrounding halo. Anthracnose diseases are typically managed through a combination of cultural practices, including pruning and removal of infected plant material, and, if necessary, the application of fungicides.

6.Red Rust (Cephaleuros virescens):

Red Rust, caused by the parasitic alga Cephaleuros virescens, is a notable disease impacting tea plants (Camellia sinensis). Characterized by distinct reddish to brownish spots on the upper surface of tea leaves, Red Rust poses a threat to the overall health and productivity of tea plantations. The affected areas often exhibit a powdery growth, giving the leaves a rusty appearance. In more severe infections, the disease can lead to leaf distortion and diminished photosynthetic efficiency. Cephaleuros virescens follows a complex life cycle, involving spore production and dissemination through wind, rain, or physical contact.

Corn and Cherry Diseases

1.Gray Leaf Spot in corn Leaf:

Gray Leaf Spot (GLS), caused by the fungus Cercospora zeae-maydis, is a significant foliar disease affecting corn (Zea mays) plants. This fungal pathogen is known for causing distinct lesions on corn leaves, which can have substantial implications for crop yield and quality. The symptoms initially appear as small, rectangular or elliptical lesions with tan centers and dark brown to grayish borders. As the disease progresses, these lesions can expand and merge, covering extensive areas of the leaves. Severe infections may lead to premature leaf senescence, affecting the plant's photosynthetic capacity and ultimately diminishing grain yield.

2.Common Rust in corn Leaf:

Common Rust (Puccinia sorghi) is a prevalent foliar disease that affects corn (Zea mays) plants, causing distinctive symptoms on the leaves. This fungal pathogen is known for the development of small, circular to elongated pustules on the upper surface of corn leaves. These pustules, often surrounded by chlorotic halos, contain masses of reddish-brown spores. Common Rust typically starts with lower leaves and progresses upward as the growing season advances.

3.Blight in corn Leaf:

Blight in corn leaves is a general term that encompasses various diseases caused by fungi, bacteria, or other pathogens, leading to the rapid and widespread death of plant tissues. One common example is Northern Corn Leaf Blight (NCLB), caused by the fungus Exserohilum turcicum. NCLB is characterized by the appearance of long, elliptical lesions with gray to tan centers and dark borders on corn leaves.

4.Cherry (including sour) Powdery mildew:

Cherry powdery mildew, which affects both sweet and sour cherry trees, is caused by the fungal pathogen Podosphaera spp. This common disease is characterized by the development of a white powdery substance on the surfaces of leaves, young shoots, and fruit. The fungus thrives in warm, dry conditions, making it a particular concern in climates with high humidity.

Other Vegetable Diseases

1.Pepper bell Bacterial spot:

Bacterial spot, caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas euvesicatoria, is a common and damaging disease affecting bell peppers (Capsicum annuum). This bacterial pathogen primarily targets the foliage, fruits, and stems of pepper plants, leading to distinctive symptoms. Infected plants display small, dark, water-soaked lesions on leaves, which may enlarge and develop a characteristic halo.

2.Lemon canker:

Lemon canker, caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas axonopodis subsp. citri, is a devastating disease affecting citrus trees, particularly lemon trees (Citrus limon). This bacterial pathogen primarily targets the leaves, stems, and fruits, leading to distinctive symptoms that can have significant economic implications for citrus cultivation. Infected lemon trees display raised, corky lesions on leaves, surrounded by a water-soaked margin, giving the appearance of cankers.
